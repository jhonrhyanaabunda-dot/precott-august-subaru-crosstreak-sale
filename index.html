01Why the Crosstrek fits Prescott

The Subaru Crosstrek just fits the Prescott driver.

Prescott sits at roughly 5,400 feet, surrounded by national forest, switchback canyon roads, and weather that swings from summer monsoons to snow on the rim. You drive on Whiskey Row for downtown errands, do long highway pulls on the 89, navigate washboard forest roads through the Prescott National Forest, and conquer a handful of snow mornings every winter. Standard symmetrical all-wheel drive and 8.7 inches of ground clearance let the Crosstrek park like a hatchback downtown but climb a fire road like a small SUV.

AWD capability is standard on every trim in the lineup. There is no front-wheel-drive version to accidentally end up in. The engine and drivetrain sit in a balanced setup, so the system reacts to a wheel losing grip faster than the part-time, reactive AWD setups you see on a lot of competing crossovers.

We will tell you honestly: the Crosstrek is not the fastest or the roomiest thing in its price range. But if you drive the terrain around Prescott, like the dirt cutoffs past Willow Creek, the dispersed-camping spots off the forest roads, or the climb toward Mingus Mountain, the Crosstrek shines in a way a softer, lower crossover cannot. It's one of our top sellers at the lot.

02New Crosstrek vs. Wilderness

Standard and Wilderness fit different lifestyles.

The standard Crosstrek is the do-everything daily driver that handles the commute, the Costco run, the trailhead, and the snow day. The Crosstrek Wilderness turns the dial toward serious off-pavement use. Picking the wrong one is the most common mistake we head off on the showroom floor.

The standard Crosstrek.

The standard Crosstrek handles the commute, the Costco run, the trailhead, and the snow day without complaint. All-wheel drive comes standard, the cabin is comfortable for two adults up front with real cargo room behind, and it slots into tight downtown Prescott parking with ease. Base and Premium trims pair the 2.0-liter boxer four-cylinder with the everyday capability most drivers here need. Stepping up adds the larger 2.5-liter engine, a bigger center touchscreen, and more comfort and convenience features. If you want a capable, fuel-efficient vehicle that covers nearly every situation a Prescott driver runs into, this is the one.

The Crosstrek Wilderness.

As per Subaru, the Wilderness adds ground clearance over the standard trim, fits Yokohama Geolandar all-terrain tires, re-gears the final drive for low-speed control, raises the roof-rail load rating for rooftop tents and gear, and adds the anti-glare hood finish and copper-accent styling that make it easy to spot in a parking lot. It's the trim to ask about if your weekends end at the end of a fire road or a dispersed campsite. The all-terrain tires and taller stance cost you a little on-road quietness and a little fuel economy versus the standard car. That's a fair price to pay if you go off-pavement, but it's a bad deal if you never leave the highway.

Not sure which fits? Our team will walk you through both without the pressure. When a couple came in last month set on the Wilderness for a daily commute that never left pavement, we walked them through both trims back to back. They left in a standard Premium. The Wilderness premium and the louder all-terrain tires were not buying them anything for the driving they did. As one reviewer put it, our staff really "explained differences between models," so you decide with clear information.

How to choose

Buy the Wilderness for the trail, the standard Crosstrek for everything else.

The Wilderness premium pays off if your weekends end at the end of a fire road: the all-terrain tires, extra clearance, re-geared final drive, and higher roof-rail load rating are real capability. If you never leave pavement, you are paying for off-road hardware in road noise and fuel economy you will not use. We would rather walk you through both, parked side by side, than sell you the wrong one and have you resent it in six months.

04Used & Certified Pre-Owned

Buying used is where the Crosstrek's reputation for going the distance pays off.

Crosstreks routinely cross 150,000 and 200,000 miles still running their original drivetrain, which is part of why a used Crosstrek holds value the way it does. The catch with any used vehicle is history, and that is exactly where we put in the work before a car ever reaches our used line.

How we vet every used Crosstrek.

Findlay Subaru Prescott is a CARFAX Top-Rated Dealer through 2024, which means vehicle history is something we hand you up front rather than something you have to dig for. On every pre-owned Crosstrek we take in, our reconditioning team pulled the full CARFAX report, inspected the drivetrain and undercarriage on the lift, and verified the AWD system and CVT operation before the car was cleared for the lot.

Where we draw the line.

When a 2022 Crosstrek came through on trade this spring with a clean body but an unexplained gap in its service history, we declined to retail it as-is and routed it to wholesale instead. We are not going to put a question mark on our own used line.

Certified Pre-Owned for the longest tail of coverage.

If you are buying used and want the longest tail of factory protection, ask about a Subaru Certified Pre-Owned Crosstrek. The Subaru CPO program adds powertrain coverage and a multi-point inspection on top of the vehicle's remaining factory warranty; the full program details and inspection checklist are published on Subaru's official certified pre-owned page. Used inventory moves fast, so the most reliable way to see current pre-owned Crosstrek availability is to check live listings or contact the team through the dealership's contact page.

06What ownership costs here

A test drive answers "Do I like it?". "Can I live with it?" is answered by the cost of ownership.

Routine maintenance is the biggest recurring line item, and we publish ours instead of hiding it: a synthetic oil change at Findlay Subaru Prescott runs $99.99, and we keep service coupons on the specials page specifically to meet or beat the independent shops around town.

Service you can document.

The argument for getting your service done here is not only the number. It is that the technician working on your Crosstrek is Subaru-trained on the exact boxer engine and CVT in your car, and the service record stays clean and dealer-documented, which is worth real money when you sell or trade.

Fuel cost, and a little altitude help.

Fuel cost is the other big lever, and the Crosstrek does well here. It is one of the more fuel-efficient AWD vehicles you can buy, and altitude helps a little: naturally aspirated boxer engines lose a touch of output above 5,000 feet but tend to run efficiently at steady highway cruise. Pull the official EPA estimates for the specific trim you are considering from fueleconomy.gov and compare the 2.0-liter against the 2.5-liter. The smaller engine returns better mileage, the larger one gives you more grunt on grades, and only you know which trade you want.

The honest caveat.

The Crosstrek is not the cheapest small crossover on the market, and the Wilderness trim costs more to buy and runs a bit thirstier on its all-terrain tires. For someone flipping cars every three years who never leaves pavement, a front-wheel-drive hatchback may be the cheaper honest answer, and we will tell you so.

What's the difference between the Crosstrek and the Crosstrek Wilderness?

The standard Crosstrek is an all-around daily driver with standard symmetrical all-wheel drive.
